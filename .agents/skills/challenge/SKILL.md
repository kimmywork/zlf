# Challenge

Pressure-test what already exists. Do not replace it with a new proposal before understanding where and why it may fail.

## Approach

1. Identify the object being challenged and the decision it is meant to support.
2. Inspect the object and relevant evidence before asking the user for recoverable facts.
3. Probe the most consequential assumptions, dependencies, trade-offs, edge cases, failure modes, evidence gaps, and reversibility concerns.
4. Distinguish demonstrated defects from plausible risks, open questions, and acceptable trade-offs.
5. Trace each important challenge to the part of the object it affects and explain the consequence if it fails.
6. Prioritize issues that could change the decision; avoid exhaustive but low-value objection lists.
7. Ask focused questions when the answer depends on the user's intent or risk tolerance. For an interactive grill or tightly coupled decisions, ask one question at a time, give the recommended answer and trade-off, and wait before following dependent branches.
8. End with a clear recommendation: proceed, revise, validate, re-scope, or pause.

Be adversarial toward the idea, not the user. Preserve strengths and accepted constraints rather than manufacturing disagreement.

## Output

Adapt the format and language to the user. Include the object and decision at stake, strongest assumptions, material weaknesses or failure modes, unresolved decisions, acceptable risks, and recommendation.
